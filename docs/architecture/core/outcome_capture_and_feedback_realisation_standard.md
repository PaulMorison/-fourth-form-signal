## Purpose

This document defines the shared platform standard for governed outcomes, governed realised feedback, governed realised-result classes, canonical outcome definitions, canonical feedback definitions, outcome identity, feedback identity, outcome semantic scope, feedback semantic scope, outcome legitimacy, feedback legitimacy, realised-result legitimacy, realised-comparison legitimacy, realised-value interpretation legitimacy, outcome lineage, feedback lineage, outcome drift, feedback drift, lifecycle posture, comparability posture, and promotion-safe feedback use across all current and future domains.

It exists because the platform now has governed standards for objective functions and optimization targets, thresholds and triggers, portfolio and policy outputs, decision case orchestration, decision state transitions, model monitoring and post-deployment drift, signal-quality and evidence-strength surfaces, training targets and labels, training data splits and evaluation protocols, policy-learning evidence admission, release readiness and promotion control, canon navigation, canon change control, end-to-end lifecycle composition, governance approval authority, shared execution deviation and outcome objects, shared post-mortem and attribution judgment, shared decision chronology, shared review resolution and disposition, and shared reopen, revisit, and reinstatement handling, but it still lacks one shared rule for how outcomes and realised feedback themselves become semantically legitimate, stable, comparable, lineage-safe, extendable, supersedable, invalidatable, and safe for repeated reuse without silently rewriting what the platform expected, silently rewriting what was actually realised, hiding negative outcomes because they are awkward to retain, or letting local feedback convenience impersonate governed reusable platform learning material.

Without such a rule, the platform will drift into outputs being treated as outcomes because they look final, realised variance being treated as causal judgment because the difference looks material, negative results being hidden because they are politically inconvenient, inherited outcome classes being locally mutated while still presented as shared platform rules, feedback records being reused across domains under stable names but different meanings, invalidated outcomes continuing to circulate because they still appear in history, and downstream monitoring, review, post-mortem, memory, and learning-adjacent interpretation resting on realised results whose meanings no longer hold still.

This document is therefore a control document for outcome capture and feedback realisation governance.

It is the canonical outcome capture and feedback realisation standard for the platform. Future governed outcomes, governed realised feedback records, governed realised-result classes, canonical outcome definitions, canonical feedback definitions, outcome-bearing decision records, realised-result-bearing case histories, feedback-bearing review packages, promotion-facing feedback consumers, and domain-local outcome and feedback extensions must align with it when preserving governed outcome, governed realised feedback, governed realised-result class, canonical outcome definition, canonical feedback definition, outcome identity, feedback identity, outcome semantic scope, feedback semantic scope, outcome legitimacy, feedback legitimacy, realised-result legitimacy, realised-comparison legitimacy, realised-value interpretation legitimacy, outcome lineage, feedback lineage, outcome drift, feedback drift, inherited outcome class, domain-extended outcome class, superseded outcome class, deprecated outcome class, retired outcome class, invalidated outcome class, comparability-safe outcome pair, non-comparable outcome pair, promotion-safe feedback use, and outcome audit trace unless a formal decision record explicitly revises it.

## Why This Standard Exists

The platform’s compounding edge depends not only on producing outputs, state transitions, orchestration paths, monitoring records, review dispositions, and post-mortem judgments, but also on disciplined control over what the platform is authorized to treat as a governed realised outcome and a governed reusable feedback signal. Outcome meaning sits between executed or non-executed decision history on one side and later interpretation, review, memory, monitoring, and learning-adjacent reuse on the other. If outcome meaning drifts quietly, the stack begins to compare, remember, and learn from records whose authority is weaker than it looks.

Outcome stability is too weak by default. An outcome can keep the same name and lose the same meaning. A realised feedback record can keep the same label and still stop expressing the same comparison claim. A negative result can remain visible in raw history and still fail governed legitimacy for reuse. A realised value interpretation can stay numerically tidy and still cease to preserve the same semantic claim. If the platform cannot state what a governed outcome means, what a governed realised feedback record means, what makes a realised result legitimate, what makes expected-versus-realised comparison legitimate, what makes realised-value interpretation legitimate, how negative outcomes remain visible without being overclaimed, and how later runs, domains, reviewers, monitors, and learning gates may compare those outcomes safely, then downstream trust weakens even while the histories still look orderly.

The platform therefore needs one shared standard so that outcomes and feedback accumulate as governed capital rather than as a pile of locally useful but semantically unstable result rows, commentary notes, retrospective labels, feedback anecdotes, awkward misses, and politically filtered historical artifacts.

comparability is not the same thing as superficial result similarity

local usefulness is not the same thing as canonical feedback admission

Outcomes may depend on output records, execution histories, decision timelines, observation horizons, review dispositions, and realised commercial evidence, but dependency does not transfer ownership. Realised feedback may inform monitoring surfaces, post-mortem review, decision memory, or policy-learning preparation, but informative reuse does not itself grant policy-learning admission, release entitlement, or dashboard authority. Realised variance may be operationally important, but variance does not inherit attribution authority merely because the difference appears consequential.

## Governed Outcome and Feedback Classes

This standard governs the shared semantic control layer that sits between already-controlled outputs, execution and chronology records, review and disposition handling, and observed commercial reality on one side and trusted reusable outcome and feedback meaning on the other.

### Local observed result

Local observed result is a locally useful observation, note, metric movement, case-specific consequence, or retrospective record that may help one operator, one case, one monitor, or one review surface without thereby qualifying for governed canonical status.

### Governed realised-result class

governed realised-result class is a governed class stating what kind of realised result is being recorded, such as favourable, negative, mixed, incomplete, deferred, exception-bearing, or otherwise bounded outcome posture, under explicit scope, explicit observation basis, explicit comparison posture, and explicit lineage strong enough that later users can tell what class claim is being made and what claim is not being made.

### Governed outcome

governed outcome is a governed post-decision result whose identity, realised-result class, semantic scope, observation basis, expected relation, and lineage are explicit enough for repeated serious reuse.

### Governed realised feedback

governed realised feedback is a governed feedback record linking expected state, realised state, comparison posture, reuse boundary, maturity posture, and lineage strongly enough that later users can tell what feedback claim it supports and what claim it does not support.

### Canonical outcome definition

canonical outcome definition is the authoritative governed definition that states what a governed outcome means, what governed realised-result class it belongs to, what semantic scope it applies to, what observation basis and expected relation support it, and what semantic conditions must remain true for reuse to stay legitimate.

### Canonical feedback definition

canonical feedback definition is the authoritative governed definition that states what governed realised feedback means, what comparison or reuse claim it supports, what semantic scope it applies to, what maturity posture and reuse boundary it requires, and what semantic conditions must remain true for reuse to stay legitimate.

Outcome classes may include favourable outcome classes, negative outcome classes, mixed outcome classes, incomplete outcome classes, deferred outcome classes, null-realisation classes, and exception-bearing outcome classes where the meaning remains explicit enough for later users to tell what kind of realised result is being claimed and what kind is not being claimed.

## Outcome Identity and Semantic Scope

### Outcome identity

outcome identity is the stable identity linking one governed outcome to its canonical outcome definition, governed realised-result class, named observation basis, named expected relation, named scope, and later lineage rather than reducing it to a case note, dashboard label, or historical row identifier.

### Outcome semantic scope

outcome semantic scope is the explicit statement of what business meaning, population meaning, decision-loop meaning, observation-horizon meaning, commercial meaning, and control boundary a governed outcome applies to and where that meaning must not be stretched by analogy or convenience.

Outcomes must have named scope, named observation basis, and named relation to prior expectation. A governed outcome that cannot state what episode, action path, non-action path, review boundary, or observation horizon it concerns, what kind of realised result it claims, what prior expected state it is being read against, and what interpretations it does not authorize is too weak for canonical reuse.

Outcome capture is only sufficient when later users can reconstruct what was expected, what was realised, what scope that result belongs to, what observation horizon governed it, and what kinds of later reuse remain in-bounds.

## Feedback Identity and Semantic Scope

### Feedback identity

feedback identity is the stable identity linking one governed realised feedback record to its canonical feedback definition, named expected-state basis, named realised-state basis, named comparison posture, named reuse boundary, and later lineage rather than reducing it to a monitor note, learning candidate label, or retrospective commentary field.

### Feedback semantic scope

feedback semantic scope is the explicit statement of what business meaning, decision-loop meaning, monitoring meaning, review meaning, memory meaning, or learning-preparatory meaning a governed realised feedback record applies to and where that meaning must not be stretched by analogy or convenience.

Realised feedback must have named source, named comparison basis, and named reuse boundary. A governed realised feedback record that cannot state what expected state it compares against, what realised evidence it depends on, what observation maturity it assumes, what downstream reuse it supports, what downstream reuse it does not support, and what contexts make it non-transferable is too weak for canonical reuse.

## Outcome and Feedback Legitimacy

### Outcome legitimacy

outcome legitimacy is the governed condition in which a governed outcome has stable identity, named scope, named realised-result class, named observation basis, named expected relation, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell what outcome claim it expresses and what outcome claim it does not express.

### Feedback legitimacy

feedback legitimacy is the governed condition in which a governed realised feedback record has stable identity, named expected-state basis, named realised-state basis, named comparison posture, named reuse boundary, named maturity posture, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell what feedback claim it expresses and what claim it does not express.

Feedback capture is legitimate only when the realised evidence, expected-state reference, observation maturity, comparison basis, and reuse boundary remain explicit enough that later users can tell why the captured feedback is fit for governed reuse and where it remains too weak.

### Realised-result legitimacy

realised-result legitimacy is the governed condition in which a realised result is classified, scoped, timed, and linked strongly enough that later users can tell why the result is being treated as favourable, negative, mixed, incomplete, deferred, or exception-bearing rather than merely present.

### Realised-comparison legitimacy

realised-comparison legitimacy is the governed condition in which expected-versus-realised comparison preserves explicit expected-state identity, explicit realised-state identity, explicit chronology, explicit observation horizon, explicit comparison basis, and explicit lineage strong enough that later users can tell why the comparison is legitimate rather than improvised.

### Realised-value interpretation legitimacy

realised-value interpretation legitimacy is the governed condition in which a governed outcome may be interpreted as value-bearing, value-destructive, value-neutral, or value-ambiguous within explicit scope and explicit observation horizon without silently importing objective authority, causal judgment authority, or policy-update authority from adjacent standards.

outcome visibility is not the same thing as outcome legitimacy. A visible result row, case note, or dashboard marker may still fail legitimacy if its realised-result class is unclear, its observation basis drifted, its expected relation is missing, or its semantic scope has quietly expanded.

negative outcome presence is not the same thing as negative outcome legitimacy. A negative outcome may still fail legitimacy if it is recorded without scope, without observation maturity, without expected relation, without lineage, or with politically distorted omission of what actually happened before the negative label was applied.

realised variance is not the same thing as attribution by itself. A gap between expected and realised results may justify careful comparison, review escalation, or post-mortem preparation and still fail to justify causal judgment until post-mortem governance performs its own work.

realised feedback is not the same thing as policy-learning admission by itself. A realised feedback record may remain legitimate for monitoring, review, memory, or post-mortem surfaces and still fail learning-admission thresholds because policy-learning governance remains a separate control role.

## Comparability and Realisation Discipline

### Outcome lineage

outcome lineage is the reconstructible chain linking outcome identity, canonical outcome definition, realised-result class, observation basis, expected relation, inherited or extended status, lifecycle status, invalidation or supersession where relevant, and later downstream use.

### Feedback lineage

feedback lineage is the reconstructible chain linking feedback identity, canonical feedback definition, expected-state basis, realised-state basis, comparison posture, reuse boundary, maturity posture, inherited or extended posture where relevant, invalidation or supersession where relevant, and later downstream use.

### Comparability-safe outcome pair

comparability-safe outcome pair is a pair of governed outcomes whose semantic scope, realised-result class, observation basis, expected relation, comparison posture, and lineage remain explicit enough that comparison is legitimate rather than inferred.

### Non-comparable outcome pair

non-comparable outcome pair is a pair of governed outcomes whose semantic scope, realised-result class, observation basis, expected relation, comparison posture, or lineage differ materially enough that comparison would mislead later users even if the outcomes share names, values, result labels, or superficially similar timelines.

### Inherited outcome class

inherited outcome class is a governed outcome class reused without material semantic change from an earlier legitimate governed outcome class whose identity and lineage remain explicit.

### Domain-extended outcome class

domain-extended outcome class is a governed outcome class that extends an inherited outcome class for a bounded domain need while keeping the extension explicit enough that comparability and semantic review remain possible.

### Promotion-safe feedback use

promotion-safe feedback use is the governed condition in which governed realised feedback may be reused in a broader domain, broader monitoring surface, broader review surface, memory surface, or policy-preparatory context without hiding local extension, outcome drift, feedback drift, negative-outcome suppression, or comparability breakage under stable names.

comparability is not the same thing as superficial result similarity. Shared labels, similar percentages, similar revenue deltas, similar colors, similar dashboards, or similarly worded case summaries do not by themselves make two governed outcomes comparable.

local usefulness is not the same thing as canonical feedback admission. One useful local feedback record may still remain non-canonical if its expected relation, realised source, observation maturity, reuse boundary, semantic scope, or lineage are too unstable, too local, or too weakly governed for serious shared reuse.

Expected-versus-realised comparison is legitimate only when the platform preserves what was expected, what was actually output, what action or non-action occurred, what chronology governed the episode, what observation horizon applied, and what realised outcome was later claimed. Realisation discipline is therefore not only about storing the realised result. It is about preserving the relation between prior expected state and later realised state strongly enough that comparison remains honest.

Inherited outcome classes must remain distinguishable from domain-extended outcome classes. Shared semantic trust fails when a local extension quietly impersonates inherited outcome meaning.

## Lifecycle Status

### Outcome drift

outcome drift is the material change in outcome meaning, realised-result classification, observation basis, expected relation, or scope that can hide underneath stable names, stable summary labels, or stable historical fields.

### Feedback drift

feedback drift is the material change in feedback comparison meaning, reuse boundary, maturity posture, expected-state basis, or realised-state basis that can hide underneath stable feedback names, stable monitor references, or stable learning-preparation labels.

### Superseded outcome class

superseded outcome class is a previously legitimate governed outcome class whose governing role has been replaced by a newer governed outcome class while preserving reconstructible historical identity and lineage.

### Deprecated outcome class

deprecated outcome class is an outcome class that remains historically recognized but is no longer approved for new ordinary use except for bounded compatibility, historical interpretation, or controlled transition purposes.

### Retired outcome class

retired outcome class is an outcome class removed from ordinary active use whose historical meaning remains preserved for lineage, audit, comparison, or retrospective interpretation.

### Invalidated outcome class

invalidated outcome class is an outcome class whose meaning, lineage, observation basis, expected relation, or surrounding control integrity has broken badly enough that serious governed reuse is no longer permitted.

### Outcome audit trace

outcome audit trace is the reconstructible trace linking outcome definitions, realised-result-class changes, expected-relation changes, observation-basis changes, comparison-basis changes, inheritance or extension, invalidation, supersession, and later downstream use.

Stable outcome and feedback labels do not guarantee stable meaning. outcome drift and feedback drift must remain explicit and auditable. Superseded outcome classes must remain historically identifiable. Deprecated outcome classes must remain visibly weaker than active outcome classes. Retired outcome classes must remain distinguishable from deprecated outcome classes. Invalidated outcome classes must remain explicitly unusable for serious governed reuse even if old tooling can still technically read them.

## Failure Modes This Standard Prevents

This standard prevents reused outcome name with changed meaning by requiring stable outcome identity, explicit semantic scope, and lineage-safe outcome changes.

This standard prevents realised feedback reused with different semantics by requiring stable feedback identity, explicit comparison posture, and explicit reuse boundary.

This standard prevents negative outcomes hidden because they are inconvenient by requiring negative-outcome visibility, explicit realised-result classes, and historically visible lifecycle posture.

This standard prevents local convenience treated as legitimacy by requiring that canonical outcome and feedback admission remain stricter than local usefulness.

This standard prevents invalidated outcomes still used as current by requiring explicit lifecycle posture, historical visibility, and block posture for invalid reuse.

This standard prevents inherited outcome class confused with domain-extended outcome class by requiring explicit inheritance posture and extension visibility.

This standard prevents non-comparable outcomes treated as equivalent by requiring comparability-safe outcome pairs and explicit non-comparable outcome pairs.

This standard prevents silent mutation of realised-result meaning by requiring explicit outcome lineage, realised-result classification discipline, and audit-ready outcome change history.

This standard prevents realised variance treated as causal judgment without legitimacy by requiring explicit separation between realised comparison, attribution judgment, and policy-learning admission.

This standard prevents lineage break between output, action, and realised outcome by requiring reconstructible linkage from expected state through realised state and later feedback reuse.

## Canonical Control Statements

Outcome meaning must remain separate from output meaning even when an output later contributes to a realised result. Outcome meaning must remain separate from state-transition meaning even when a case moves cleanly through governed states. Outcome meaning must remain separate from orchestration meaning even when one episode design materially shaped what could later be realised. Realised feedback must remain separate from monitoring ownership, post-mortem judgment ownership, learning-admission authority, release authority, and dashboard authority even when all of those surfaces consume the same historical records.

Outcome legitimacy requires explicit identity, explicit semantic scope, explicit realised-result class, explicit observation basis, explicit expected relation, and lineage strong enough for serious reuse. Feedback legitimacy requires explicit identity, explicit expected-state basis, explicit realised-state basis, explicit comparison posture, explicit maturity posture, explicit reuse boundary, and lineage strong enough for serious reuse. Realised-comparison legitimacy requires that expected-versus-realised comparison be reconstructible rather than narrated after the fact. Realised-value interpretation legitimacy requires that value interpretation remain explicit and bounded rather than becoming a back door for objective or causal reinterpretation.

Negative outcomes must remain visible. Mixed outcomes must remain distinguishable from negative outcomes. Incomplete outcomes must remain distinguishable from unresolved or deferred outcomes. Silent outcome suppression is unacceptable. Silent feedback repurposing is unacceptable. Silent comparison-basis changes are unacceptable. Historical visibility is required for serious reuse.

## Implementation Expectations

Every governed outcome, governed realised feedback record, and governed realised-result class must preserve a canonical definition, stable identity, explicit semantic scope, intended audience where relevant, named expected relation, named observation basis, named maturity posture where relevant, named lifecycle status, and lineage strong enough for later reconstruction.

Every material outcome change, realised-result-class change, observation-basis change, expected-relation change, comparison-basis change, feedback reuse-boundary change, inheritance change, or lifecycle-status change must remain audit-ready and reviewable rather than being treated as harmless historical maintenance.

Every output-bearing package, action or non-action record, chronology surface, review package, memory package, monitoring surface, post-mortem package, or learning-preparatory package that can materially shape realised interpretation must preserve enough traceability that later users can tell what was expected, what was output, what action or non-action occurred, what observation horizon applied, what outcome class was recorded, what feedback comparison was made, what lifecycle status it carried, and what changes materially affected meaning.

Negative outcomes must not be hidden by omission, soft deletion, dashboard filtering, summary rewriting, or case-closure convenience. Where outcome capture is incomplete, the incompleteness itself must remain explicit rather than being laundered into neutral or favorable interpretation.

Human review must remain available where outcome or feedback changes materially alter cross-domain comparability, learning-preparatory interpretation, customer-impact interpretation, or serious historical accountability even when the numeric change looks small.

## Governance and Approval

Consequential outcome and feedback changes must follow the canon change-control and quality-gate standard, the canon navigation and reading-order standard, the end-to-end decision lifecycle composition standard, and the platform governance roles and approval authority matrix rather than entering the canon by convenience. Approval authority does not arise from dashboard ownership, monitoring ownership, post-mortem participation, or local familiarity with one outcome dataset. Where outcome or feedback changes materially alter shared platform meaning, shared historical comparability, or shared learning-preparatory interpretation, the relevant architecture and governance authorities must remain able to review and approve those changes explicitly.

Canonical outcome-class admission, realised-feedback admission, supersession, deprecation, retirement, invalidation, and major comparison-basis changes must preserve explicit approval lineage strong enough that later contributors can tell who approved the change, under what governance role, and why the change was treated as legitimate rather than merely convenient.

## Closing Position

Outcome capture and feedback realisation governance must remain a first-class platform control whose outcome meaning, realised-result legitimacy, expected-versus-realised comparison legitimacy, feedback reuse boundaries, negative-outcome visibility, lifecycle posture, and drift visibility remain explicit enough that the platform can learn seriously from realised history without mistaking visible records, visible variances, visible negative results, or visible dashboards for governed legitimacy. Outcomes are powerful precisely because they feel retrospective and concrete. That apparent concreteness makes silent drift, political filtering, and boundary laundering especially dangerous. The platform must therefore govern outcome-and-feedback meaning directly, preserve historical visibility directly, and refuse uncontrolled feedback sprawl even when local convenience argues otherwise.
