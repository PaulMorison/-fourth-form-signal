# Signal Quality and Evidence Strength Surface Standard for the Fourth Form Retail Decision Intelligence Platform

## Purpose

This document defines the shared platform standard for governed signal-quality surfaces and governed evidence-strength surfaces across all current and future domains.

It exists because the platform now has governed standards for evidence bundles and signal provenance, uncertainty and confidence context, rationale trace, briefing and digest surfaces, canonical metrics and KPIs, decision surfaces and dashboards, policy-learning evidence admission, canon navigation, canon change control, lifecycle composition, and governance authority, but it still lacks one shared rule for how signal-quality surfaces and evidence-strength surfaces become semantically legitimate, comparable, lineage-safe, derivation-safe, extendable, supersedable, invalidatable, and safe for repeated reuse without silently redefining evidence meaning, silently redefining recommendation legitimacy, or drifting into dashboard theater and cosmetic trust.

Without such a rule, the platform will drift into useful local score surfaces being treated as governed simply because they help one audience once, score visibility being mistaken for meaning, derived evidence-strength displays being treated as if they were evidence bundles, uncertainty labels being mistaken for evidence-strength surfaces, inherited surfaces being locally mutated while still presented as shared platform rules, invalidated surfaces continuing to circulate because they still exist in old views, and downstream recommendations, reviews, dashboards, and learning work resting on score surfaces whose meaning no longer holds still.

This document is therefore a control document for signal-quality and evidence-strength surface governance.

It is the canonical signal quality and evidence strength surface standard for the platform. Future governed signal-quality surfaces, governed evidence-strength surfaces, canonical surface definitions, derived score-bearing surfaces, review-facing surface derivatives, briefing-facing signal summaries, dashboard-consumed signal-quality views, and domain-local surface extensions must align with it when preserving governed signal-quality surface, governed evidence-strength surface, canonical surface definition, surface identity, surface semantic scope, signal-quality legitimacy, evidence-strength legitimacy, derivation legitimacy, signal class, evidence-strength class, inherited surface, domain-extended surface, surface lineage, surface drift, semantic drift, comparability-safe surface pair, non-comparable surface pair, superseded surface, deprecated surface, retired surface, invalidated surface, promotion-safe surface use, and surface audit trace unless a formal decision record explicitly revises it.

This standard governs governed signal-quality surfaces, governed evidence-strength surfaces, canonical surface definitions, surface identity, surface semantic scope, audience-appropriate surface meaning, surface legitimacy, derivation legitimacy, surface inputs, surface lineage, signal classes, evidence-strength classes, cross-surface comparability, inherited and domain-extended surfaces, surface drift visibility, semantic drift visibility, lifecycle status of governed surfaces, promotion-safe surface use, and boundary separation from evidence bundles, uncertainty context, rationale traces, dashboards, metrics, briefings, and policy-learning admission.

## Why This Standard Exists

The platform's compounding edge depends not only on preserving evidence, uncertainty, rationale, metrics, views, and learning gates, but also on disciplined control over the derived surfaces that communicate signal quality and evidence strength to humans and downstream systems. These surfaces sit between underlying controlled objects and later interpretation. If their meaning drifts quietly, the stack begins to trust surfaces whose semantic authority is weaker than it looks.

Surface stability is too weak by default. A surface can keep the same name and lose the same meaning. A scale can keep the same labels and still stop representing the same interpretation. A derived class can still look intuitive and still fail legitimacy. If the platform cannot state what a governed signal-quality surface means, what a governed evidence-strength surface means, what derivation basis still supports those surfaces, what scope and audience still constrain them, and how later runs, domains, stores, models, outputs, or views may compare them safely, then downstream trust weakens even while the surface still looks orderly.

The platform therefore needs one shared standard so that signal-quality and evidence-strength surfaces accumulate as governed capital rather than as a pile of locally useful but semantically unstable score cards, trust indicators, evidence-strength bands, and view-local convenience labels.

## Governed Signal-Quality and Evidence-Strength Surfaces

This standard governs the shared control layer that sits between upstream controlled objects on one side and trusted derived signal-quality and evidence-strength surfaces on the other.

### Governed signal-quality surface

governed signal-quality surface is a derived governed surface that states how trustworthy, traceable, current, complete, coherent, or otherwise usable an upstream signal basis appears within explicit scope, explicit derivation basis, explicit interpretation, and explicit lineage strong enough for repeated serious use.

### Governed evidence-strength surface

governed evidence-strength surface is a derived governed surface that states how materially strong or weak an evidence basis appears for a bounded interpretive question within explicit scope, explicit derivation basis, explicit interpretation, and explicit lineage strong enough for repeated serious use.

### Canonical surface definition

canonical surface definition is the authoritative governed definition that states what a governed signal-quality surface or governed evidence-strength surface means, what semantic scope it applies to, what derivation basis it depends on, what classes it may express, what audiences it may support, and what semantic conditions must remain true for reuse to stay legitimate.

These governed surfaces may communicate derived judgments about traceability, freshness, coherence, bounded sufficiency, and interpretive strength, but they do not become substitute evidence bundles, substitute uncertainty records, substitute rationale traces, substitute recommendations, or substitute learning-admission judgments merely because they are useful to look at.

## Surface Identity, Scope, and Audience

### Surface identity

surface identity is the stable identity linking one governed surface to its canonical surface definition, semantic scope, derivation basis, interpretation, audience posture, and later lineage rather than reducing it to a screen label, score name, or local alias.

### Surface semantic scope

surface semantic scope is the explicit statement of what business meaning, decision-support meaning, evidence-support meaning, audience purpose, and control boundary a governed surface applies to and where that meaning must not be stretched by analogy or convenience.

Surfaces must have named scope, derivation basis, and interpretation. A governed surface that cannot state what question it is answering, what input basis it depends on, what audience it is for, and what it does not prove is too weak for canonical reuse.

Audience does matter, but audience fitting does not grant semantic freedom. A surface may be rendered in a briefing, a review aid, an operational view, or another permitted surface, yet the underlying governed meaning must remain stable enough that later users can tell what the surface means and what it does not mean.

## Surface Legitimacy and Semantic Boundaries

### Signal-quality legitimacy

signal-quality legitimacy is the governed condition in which a signal-quality surface has stable identity, named scope, named derivation basis, named interpretation, explicit audience posture, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell what kind of signal-quality judgment it expresses and what judgment it does not express.

### Evidence-strength legitimacy

evidence-strength legitimacy is the governed condition in which an evidence-strength surface has stable identity, named scope, named derivation basis, named interpretation, explicit audience posture, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell what kind of evidence-strength judgment it expresses and what judgment it does not express.

score visibility is not the same thing as semantic legitimacy. A visible surface may still be semantically illegitimate if the derivation basis is unclear, the interpretation drifted, the audience changed silently, the score labels were reused under different meaning, or the surface began carrying claims that belong to another standard.

surface presence is not the same thing as surface legitimacy. A surface that exists in software, reports, notebooks, or dashboards without preserved semantic legitimacy remains too weak for serious reuse.

signal-quality surfaces must not silently redefine evidence meaning. evidence-strength surfaces must not silently redefine recommendation legitimacy. A surface may qualify, orient, or summarize trust in a bounded way, but it must not silently take over object meaning that belongs elsewhere.

## Surface Inputs, Lineage, and Derivation Discipline

### Derivation legitimacy

derivation legitimacy is the governed condition in which the inputs, transformations, class assignments, interpretation rules, and audience-safe reductions used to create a governed surface remain explicit enough that later users can reconstruct why the derived surface was legitimate.

### Surface lineage

surface lineage is the reconstructible chain linking surface identity, canonical surface definition, upstream controlled inputs, derivation basis, class assignments, lifecycle status, invalidation or supersession where relevant, and later downstream use.

### Surface audit trace

surface audit trace is the reconstructible trace linking surface definition, input references, derivation rules, interpretation rules, label changes, class changes, inheritance or extension, invalidation, supersession, and later downstream use.

Derivation changes must remain explicit and lineage-safe. A surface may depend on evidence bundles, signal provenance, uncertainty context, rationale traces, metrics, or other controlled inputs where relevant, but those inputs must remain named strongly enough that the platform can still tell what was derived from what and under which interpretive rule.

Local score rendering, banding, color-coding, or summary packaging must remain subordinate to derivation legitimacy. Local scoring convenience is not the same thing as governed surface meaning.

## Signal Quality Classes

### Signal class

signal class is the governed class used by a signal-quality surface to express the interpreted quality posture of the upstream signal basis within the surface's declared scope and derivation basis.

Signal classes are canonical interpretation classes, not free-floating design labels and not metric ownership by another name.

### Traceable signal class

traceable signal class is the surface condition in which upstream signal provenance, freshness posture, coherence posture, and derivation basis remain explicit enough that the surface may state strong signal-quality meaning within its declared scope.

### Qualified signal class

qualified signal class is the surface condition in which the upstream signal basis remains useful but materially bounded by freshness, completeness, coherence, traceability, or scope limits that the surface must keep explicit.

### Degraded signal class

degraded signal class is the surface condition in which the upstream signal basis remains visible but materially weakened strongly enough that later users must not interpret the surface as if strong signal-quality meaning still holds.

### Invalid signal class

invalid signal class is the surface condition in which the upstream signal basis no longer supports legitimate current signal-quality interpretation strongly enough for governed reuse.

## Evidence Strength Classes

### Evidence-strength class

evidence-strength class is the governed class used by an evidence-strength surface to express the interpreted strength posture of the upstream evidence basis within the surface's declared scope and derivation basis.

Evidence-strength classes are canonical interpretation classes for surfaces, not automatic recommendation permissions, not uncertainty ownership, and not learning-admission thresholds.

### Strong evidence-strength class

strong evidence-strength class is the surface condition in which the upstream evidence basis appears materially strong enough for the bounded interpretive question the surface declares, while remaining subordinate to the underlying evidence, uncertainty, and rationale controls.

### Qualified evidence-strength class

qualified evidence-strength class is the surface condition in which the upstream evidence basis supports bounded interpretation but remains materially limited by conflict, incompleteness, freshness, attribution, or scope conditions that the surface must keep explicit.

### Weak evidence-strength class

weak evidence-strength class is the surface condition in which the upstream evidence basis remains visible but too weak for strong interpretive claims within the declared scope.

### Unsupported evidence-strength class

unsupported evidence-strength class is the surface condition in which the upstream evidence basis no longer supports legitimate current evidence-strength interpretation strongly enough for governed reuse.

## Comparability, Reuse, and Cross-Surface Integrity

### Comparability-safe surface pair

comparability-safe surface pair is a pair of governed surfaces whose semantic scope, derivation basis, interpretation, class schema, audience posture where relevant, and lineage remain explicit enough that comparison is legitimate rather than inferred.

### Non-comparable surface pair

non-comparable surface pair is a pair of governed surfaces whose semantic scope, derivation basis, interpretation, class schema, audience posture, or lineage differ materially enough that comparison must remain blocked or explicitly qualified.

### Inherited surface

inherited surface is a governed surface reused without material semantic change from an earlier legitimate surface whose identity and lineage remain explicit.

### Domain-extended surface

domain-extended surface is a governed surface that extends an inherited surface for a bounded domain need while keeping the extension explicit enough that comparability and semantic review remain possible.

comparability is not the same thing as superficial score similarity. Shared labels, similar score bands, similar colors, similar layouts, or similar audiences do not by themselves make two surfaces comparable.

Inherited surfaces must remain distinguishable from domain-extended surfaces. A domain extension may narrow scope or add bounded local interpretation, but it must not silently widen meaning, silently change derivation basis, or silently reuse the inherited label as if shared meaning remained unchanged.

Cross-surface reuse must preserve derivation legitimacy, interpretation legitimacy, and lineage strongly enough that one surface does not become a stealth substitute for another just because it feels familiar.

## Drift, Invalidation, and Supersession

### Surface drift

surface drift is the governed condition in which a surface's practical behavior, displayed classes, derivation basis, audience posture, or interpretive consequence shifts materially enough that later reuse may no longer be semantically safe.

### Semantic drift

semantic drift is the governed condition in which a surface keeps the same visible label or apparent shape while the meaning of its classes, derivation rules, or interpretation changes materially underneath it.

### Superseded surface

superseded surface is a surface whose current canonical role has been replaced by a later governed surface while its historical identity remains visible and reconstructible.

### Deprecated surface

deprecated surface is a surface whose new use is discouraged or bounded while its historical identity and limited transitional visibility remain active.

### Retired surface

retired surface is a surface whose active governed use has ended while its historical existence and semantic trace remain reconstructible.

### Invalidated surface

invalidated surface is a surface whose ordinary reuse is prohibited because signal-quality legitimacy, evidence-strength legitimacy, derivation legitimacy, comparability posture, or lineage posture has been broken materially enough that governed reuse is unsafe.

### Promotion-safe surface use

promotion-safe surface use is surface use whose identity, scope, derivation basis, interpretation, lifecycle status, and lineage are explicit enough that it may be considered through stricter downstream gates without implying that broader canonical admission or unrestricted reuse has already been granted.

surface drift must remain explicit and reviewable. superseded surfaces must remain historically identifiable. retired surfaces must remain distinguishable from deprecated surfaces.

surface usefulness is not the same thing as canonical admission. Canonical surface admission must be stricter than local presentation usefulness, and promotion-safe surface use must be stricter than local display convenience.

## Failure Modes and Anti-Patterns

### Reused surface name with changed meaning

A surface name may remain stable while scope, derivation, interpretation, or class meaning changes underneath it. That breaks legitimacy while falsely preserving apparent continuity.

### Evidence-strength scale reused with different semantics

An evidence-strength scale may survive across contexts even though the derivation basis or interpretive question changed materially. That preserves familiarity while destroying semantic comparability.

### Signal-quality score reused across non-comparable contexts

A signal-quality score may be reused across domains, models, stores, or outputs as though one visible score shape proved comparability, even when the underlying surface pair is non-comparable.

### Inherited surface mistaken for domain-extended surface

Local extension may quietly impersonate inherited surface meaning, causing later users to assume shared surface semantics where only bounded local adaptation exists.

### Cosmetic trust mistaken for semantic legitimacy

Users may trust a surface because it looks polished, intuitive, or stable even though the surface's derivation basis, lineage, or interpretation is too weak for serious trust.

### Surface drift hidden under stable labels

Visible labels and score bands may remain unchanged while meaning drifts materially underneath them, making stable naming a disguise for unstable semantics.

### Invalidated surface still used as current

An invalidated surface may remain active in software, reports, or habits even after governance has withdrawn it, leaving obsolete surface meaning in circulation.

### Local usefulness mistaken for canonical legitimacy

One useful local surface may be treated as though repeated convenience proved governed canonical validity. That confuses presentation utility with platform control.

### Lineage break between upstream evidence and derived surface

The surface may lose reconstructible linkage back to its evidence or signal basis, leaving later users unable to tell why the surface ever looked legitimate.

### Silent mutation of score interpretation

Bands, labels, thresholds, or interpretive notes may change without explicit governance visibility, causing readers to trust a stable shape whose meaning no longer matches prior use.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

Any surface that violates this standard loses claim to governed canonical trust until the relevant defect is corrected or the surface is formally invalidated, superseded, deprecated, retired, or otherwise constrained by explicit governance.

Where non-compliance materially affects recommendation handling, review handling, dashboard trust, metric interpretation, or learning-adjacent interpretation, the platform must treat that defect as a governance problem rather than as a cosmetic presentation issue. Reuse may be blocked. Promotion may be blocked. Comparative use may be blocked. Downstream consumers may be required to step down into the underlying controlled objects rather than relying on the surface.

If the defect created semantic ambiguity strong enough that later users cannot tell what the surface meant, surface legitimacy is broken and the surface must not continue as if it were still current merely because software still renders it.

## Change Management Notes

Changes to canonical surface definitions, class schemas, derivation rules, scope declarations, audience posture, lifecycle status, comparability conditions, or promotion-safe use boundaries are consequential canon changes and must align with the canon change-control and quality-gate standard at the stricter applicable path.

Future signal-quality-surface extensions must be placed according to control role, not convenience. Shared surface-meaning control belongs here. Shared object meaning belongs in the relevant object standards. View design and recurring dashboard meaning belong in dashboard governance. Metric meaning belongs in metric governance. Learning-admission thresholds belong in policy-learning governance. Surface-related additions that cannot name their control role clearly are not ready for canonical entry.

Consequential revisions must preserve supersession, deprecation, retirement, invalidation, and memory visibility strongly enough that later contributors can reconstruct what changed and why. Governance-visible approval must follow the live authority matrix rather than local implementation preference.
