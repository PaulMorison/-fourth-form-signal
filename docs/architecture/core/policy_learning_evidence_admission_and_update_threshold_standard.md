# Policy-Learning Evidence Admission and Update-Threshold Standard for the Fourth Form Retail Decision Intelligence Platform

## Purpose of This Document

This document defines the shared platform standard for admitting evidence into policy learning and for deciding when that evidence is strong enough to justify policy update.

It exists because the platform cannot learn safely if every domain decides for itself what counts as learning-grade evidence, when repeated cases are comparable enough to combine, when attribution is strong enough to support adaptation, or when a proposed update is still too weak, too local, too recent, too scope-blurred, or too politically distorted to govern future behavior.

Without a shared standard, the platform will drift into domain-specific evidence thresholds, policy updates from incomplete decision episodes, cross-scope learning that is not governed, adaptation from anecdotal overrides, updates triggered by outcome headlines without attribution discipline, weak distinction between evidence admission and policy update approval, and policy-learning behavior that becomes more active while becoming less trustworthy.

This document is therefore a control document for policy-learning evidence admission and update-threshold discipline.

It defines the shared concepts, evidence-admission rules, update-threshold rules, evidence-quality expectations, scope checks, inheritance rules, extension rules, and governance linkage that all domains must follow before governed policy behavior may change.

It is the canonical shared policy-learning evidence admission and update-threshold document for the platform. Future domains, policy-learning contracts, adaptation thresholds, and evidence-to-update rules must align with it unless a formal decision record explicitly revises it.

## Role of This Document in the Platform

This document governs the shared gate between post-decision learning signals and actual policy change.

The shared decision case and decision memory standard defines how decision episodes are anchored and preserved. The shared execution deviation and outcome standard defines the structured back-half evidence. The shared override and approval standard preserves human intervention. The shared post-mortem standard defines attribution grammar and evidence-quality judgment. Domain-local policy-learning contracts define how a domain adapts within its business logic. This document governs the platform-level rule for when those artifacts are admissible as learning evidence and when accumulated evidence is strong enough to justify changing future policy behavior.

In practical terms, this document governs five things.

- What counts as admissible evidence for policy learning.
- What conditions make a set of cases comparable enough for governed learning.
- What threshold must be met before evidence may justify policy change.
- How insufficient evidence must block or defer adaptation.
- How all domains must inherit one shared evidence-to-update discipline.

This document therefore governs policy-learning gate discipline as part of platform coherence.

## Core Thesis

In the Fourth Form platform, policy must change only when governed decision-loop evidence is admissible, comparable, scope-valid, attribution-strong enough, and repeated enough to justify adaptation, so that future behavior improves through disciplined learning rather than through reactive adjustment to noise, local anecdotes, or politically salient cases.

That is the core thesis.

Evidence admission and update threshold are not the same question. Evidence may be worth remembering before it is strong enough to justify policy change.

## What This Standard Is and Is Not

This standard is the shared platform rule for deciding which evidence may enter governed policy-learning review and when that evidence base is strong enough to support actual policy adaptation.

It is not any of the following.

- It is not a generic machine-learning retraining trigger.
- It is not permission for domains to change policy because recent outcomes look directionally interesting.
- It is not a substitute for domain-local policy logic, workflow design, or simulation design.
- It is not a claim that every complete post-mortem automatically supports adaptation.
- It is not a rule that treats evidence admission as automatic policy-update approval.
- It is not a way to launder weak narrative judgments into governing future behavior.

A real shared evidence-admission and update-threshold standard means the platform can answer the following questions for any proposed policy change.

- Which governed artifacts support the proposed learning claim.
- Whether those artifacts are complete enough to admit into policy-learning review.
- Whether the cases are comparable in commercial and scope meaning.
- Whether attribution and evidence quality are strong enough for adaptation.
- Whether the proposed update is justified now, should be deferred, or should be rejected.

## Why a Shared Evidence-Admission and Threshold Standard Is Necessary

Domains must not define policy-learning admission and threshold rules independently because the platform cannot compound intelligence coherently if one domain updates policy from thin evidence while another waits for governed repeated proof.

If admission and threshold logic are left local, several failures follow.

- Policy-learning contracts stop meaning the same thing across domains.
- Evidence strength becomes politically negotiable rather than structurally governed.
- Override patterns are overlearned in one domain and ignored in another.
- Cross-domain confidence in policy adaptation collapses because update discipline is inconsistent.
- Future implementation work begins inferring learning legitimacy from local habits rather than from shared platform rules.

The platform therefore needs one shared standard so that every domain can extend one governed learning gate rather than inventing its own threshold culture.

## Core Concepts

The platform uses the following core concepts.

### Evidence admission

Evidence admission is the governed decision that a set of policy-learning inputs is complete, interpretable, scope-valid, and structurally fit to enter learning review.

### Admissible evidence

Admissible evidence is governed decision-loop evidence that satisfies the minimum completeness, lineage, scope, and attribution conditions required for policy-learning consideration.

### Update threshold

Update threshold is the governed standard of strength that the admitted evidence base must meet before a policy update may be accepted.

### Learning-grade evidence

Learning-grade evidence is admitted evidence whose quality, comparability, repetition, and commercial significance are strong enough to support adaptation rather than mere memory or monitoring.

### Evidence sufficiency

Evidence sufficiency is the judgment about whether the available admitted evidence is strong enough to support the specific kind of policy change being proposed.

### Comparable case set

A comparable case set is a governed collection of decision episodes whose scope, commercial meaning, operating conditions, and attribution structure are sufficiently aligned to support joint learning.

### Weak-evidence protection

Weak-evidence protection is the rule set that blocks policy adaptation from noisy, incomplete, immature, contradictory, unrepresentative, or politically distorted evidence.

### Update severity

Update severity is the practical magnitude of the proposed policy change, including how broadly it applies, how strongly it alters action behavior, and how much governance sensitivity it carries.

### Scope-valid learning

Scope-valid learning is learning performed only from evidence whose learning scope, tenant boundary, banner or brand validity, and transfer legitimacy are explicitly authorized.

### Deferred adaptation

Deferred adaptation is the governed outcome in which evidence is admitted into monitoring or memory but is judged not yet strong enough to justify current policy change.

## Shared Evidence-Admission Model

At platform level, evidence admission is the formal gate that determines whether a policy-learning input set is fit for governed consideration.

It exists because the platform must distinguish between evidence that is worth preserving and evidence that is strong enough even to be reviewed for adaptation.

The shared evidence-admission model must contain, conceptually, all of the following.

- A defined proposed learning claim or candidate update direction.
- Links to the originating decision cases and relevant memory objects where available.
- The recommendation, approval, override, execution, outcome, and post-mortem lineage needed to interpret the cases being used.
- Explicit learning-scope references and any relevant banner, brand, store, client, or network boundaries.
- An evidence-quality position strong enough to separate structurally interpretable evidence from weak narrative impression.
- A comparability judgment stating whether the cases belong in one learning set.
- A decision stating whether the evidence is admitted, admitted with restrictions, deferred, or rejected for policy-learning review.

The evidence-admission model is therefore not a casual note that interesting outcomes exist. It is the governed gate into policy-learning consideration.

## Shared Update-Threshold Model

At platform level, update threshold is the formal rule by which admitted evidence is judged strong enough, or not strong enough, to justify policy change.

It exists because admission alone does not prove that the platform should alter future action behavior.

The shared update-threshold model must contain, conceptually, all of the following.

- A statement of what policy behavior is proposed to change.
- The severity and scope of that proposed change.
- The admitted evidence base supporting that change.
- An evidence sufficiency judgment matched to the severity of the proposed update.
- A weak-evidence check stating what competing explanations or failure modes remain unresolved.
- A decision stating whether the update is accepted, deferred pending more evidence, narrowed to a more local scope, or rejected.

The update-threshold model is therefore not a simple count threshold detached from context. It is the governed strength test for adaptation.

## Evidence Admission Rules

Evidence may enter governed policy-learning review only when the following rules are satisfied.

### Complete decision-loop lineage

The evidence set must preserve reconstructible linkage among the decision case, recommendation path, approval and override state where relevant, execution deviation, realized outcome, and post-mortem judgment.

### Scope validity

The evidence must be inside an explicitly authorized learning scope. Learning scope, decision scope, and reporting scope must remain distinct.

### Attribution readiness

The evidence must include post-mortem judgment or equivalent governed attribution strong enough to distinguish recommendation weakness, execution weakness, override effect, environmental change, information gap, or insufficient evidence.

### Observation maturity

The relevant observation horizon must be mature enough for the learning claim being considered. Early outcome signals may be remembered while still being inadmissible for adaptation.

### Commercial interpretability

The cases must be interpretable in commercially meaningful terms rather than as abstract statistical movement alone.

### Comparable-case discipline

The evidence set must not combine cases whose operating conditions, banner logic, brand logic, store reality, or decision meaning are too different for one governed learning claim.

### Override discipline

Override evidence is admissible only when the original recommendation, the changed approved path, the realized execution, and the later post-mortem consequence are all preserved strongly enough to interpret the override seriously.

### Boundary-safe transfer discipline

Evidence that crosses store, group, client, banner, brand, or domain boundaries may be admitted only when the transfer logic is explicitly governed and commercially defensible.

Evidence that fails these rules may still be preserved in decision memory, but it is not admissible as governed policy-learning evidence.

## Update-Threshold Rules

Admitted evidence justifies policy update only when the threshold for the proposed change is met.

At minimum, the following rules apply.

### Stronger change requires stronger evidence

The broader or more behavior-changing the proposed update is, the stronger and more repeated the evidence must be.

### Repetition matters unless severity is exceptional

Most policy updates require repeated comparable evidence rather than one memorable case. A single case may justify urgent restricted action only when it exposes a high-severity structural failure that governance would treat as unsafe to ignore.

### Attribution strength must match update ambition

If the platform cannot distinguish recommendation weakness from execution weakness, override effect, or regime change with adequate confidence, the threshold for policy update is not met.

### Local contradiction limits broad transfer

When local evidence repeatedly contradicts a broader network pattern, broader adaptation must be narrowed, deferred, or rejected rather than forced into local policy.

### Weak evidence permits monitoring before adaptation

Evidence may justify watchlist placement, additional data collection, simulation review, or domain-local investigation even when it does not justify policy change.

### Commercial significance matters

An update threshold is not met merely because a pattern is statistically or narratively noticeable. The pattern must matter enough to decision quality, risk, feasibility, or commercial usefulness to justify changing governed behavior.

### Governance sensitivity matters

An update that materially alters learning scope, decision branching, explanation behavior, override handling, or cross-banner transfer requires a higher threshold and stronger review discipline than a narrow calibration refinement.

## Evidence Sufficiency Dimensions

Evidence sufficiency should be judged across several dimensions rather than as one crude numeric test.

At minimum, the platform should judge the following.

### Completeness

Are the relevant case, recommendation, override, execution, outcome, and post-mortem artifacts present and linked?

### Consistency

Do repeated cases point in a materially similar direction rather than producing a fragile mixed story?

### Comparability

Are the cases commercially and operationally similar enough to support one learning claim?

### Attribution quality

Is the attribution judgment strong enough to support the type of update proposed?

### Scope legitimacy

Is the learning claim being made only within a valid learning boundary?

### Transfer validity

If the update will apply beyond the observed cases, is that transfer commercially and structurally defensible?

### Magnitude alignment

Does the strength of evidence justify the size and breadth of the proposed policy change?

The platform should treat evidence sufficiency as a governed synthesis across these dimensions, not as a single unqualified pass mark.

## Evidence Admission Outcomes

Evidence-admission review should produce one of the following governed outcomes.

### Admitted for update consideration

The evidence is complete, interpretable, scope-valid, and strong enough to enter update-threshold review.

### Admitted with restrictions

The evidence is fit for learning review only within a narrower scope, a narrower commercial interpretation, or a more limited candidate-update class.

### Deferred pending more evidence

The evidence is structurally meaningful but not yet mature, repeated, or attributable enough for update-threshold review.

### Rejected for learning use

The evidence is too incomplete, too scope-invalid, too weakly attributable, or too incomparable to be treated as governed policy-learning evidence.

These outcomes matter because the platform should not collapse every interesting signal into the binary of update now or ignore forever.

## Update Decision Outcomes

After threshold review, a candidate policy update should produce one of the following governed outcomes.

### Accepted

The evidence base is strong enough to justify the proposed update within the approved scope.

### Accepted with narrowed scope

The evidence supports adaptation only for a more local, banner-specific, client-specific, or otherwise restricted policy scope.

### Deferred for continued monitoring

The evidence is important but still insufficient for change; the platform should continue monitoring, accumulate more comparable cases, or strengthen simulation and workflow review.

### Rejected

The proposed update is not justified by the admitted evidence base.

These decision outcomes keep policy-learning behavior explicit and reconstructible.

## Scope and Transfer Rules

The platform must not use policy-learning thresholds to blur scope boundaries.

The following rules apply.

- Learning scope must be explicit before evidence is admitted.
- Reporting entitlement does not determine learning admissibility.
- Broader learning rights do not automatically justify broader policy transfer.
- Cross-banner and cross-brand transfer is invalid by default and requires specific commercial and governance justification.
- Network evidence may inform broad priors while still failing the threshold for direct local overwrite.
- Domain-local evidence may justify local policy adaptation without justifying platform-wide change.

Policy-learning legitimacy depends not only on evidence quality but also on valid scope discipline.

## Override and Human-Intervention Evidence Rules

Human intervention is important learning evidence, but it is not self-validating.

The following rules apply.

- Frequent override is not automatic proof of policy weakness.
- One successful override is not automatic proof of a structural blind spot.
- Override evidence is update-grade only when repeated override patterns, realized outcomes, and post-mortem judgments point to the same underlying issue.
- Override-triggered updates must preserve the distinction between missing context, execution feasibility, human preference, and true recommendation weakness.
- Politically salient override behavior must not bypass the normal evidence-admission and update-threshold gates.

Override matters because it can reveal real blind spots, but only governed evidence can show when that is actually true.

## Governance Linkage

Changes to policy-learning evidence admission or update-threshold rules are consequential governance events.

Because these rules determine how the platform changes itself over time, revisions to shared admission logic, shared update-threshold logic, cross-scope learning transfer, or evidence sufficiency discipline must be treated as shared-platform changes rather than as local wording adjustments.

Under the platform governance authority matrix, policy-learning rule changes require governance and boundary review and domain authority approval where relevant. If a proposed change affects shared platform behavior across domains, platform-level approval discipline applies.

This standard therefore links directly to governance authority, decision-record change control, tenant-boundary discipline, shared attribution grammar, and domain-local policy-learning contracts.

## Domain Inheritance Rules

All admitted domains must inherit this shared evidence-admission and update-threshold discipline.

At minimum, every domain policy-learning contract must align with the following.

- Admitted evidence must come from governed decision-loop artifacts.
- Learning scope must remain explicit and boundary-safe.
- Attribution quality must be judged before adaptation occurs.
- Update threshold must be matched to update severity.
- Weak-evidence protection must block anecdotal or noisy adaptation.
- Deferred adaptation must remain available as a governed outcome.

Domains may implement stricter local thresholds, but they may not weaken these shared requirements while still claiming governed platform alignment.

## Domain Extension Rules

Domains may extend this standard where their operating reality requires more specific admission or threshold rules.

Valid domain extension may include the following.

- Additional evidence-quality dimensions specific to the domain's operating mechanics.
- Stronger maturity requirements for slow-developing outcomes.
- Narrower transfer rules for domains with highly heterogeneous local conditions.
- More specific update classes and matched threshold tests.

Domain extension is invalid when it does any of the following.

- Redefines admissible evidence to include thin narrative impressions in place of governed artifacts.
- Treats evidence admission as equivalent to update approval.
- Removes the distinction between local, network, banner, brand, tenant, or client learning validity.
- Lowers update threshold merely to make adaptation appear faster or more autonomous.
- Allows one domain's convenience to redefine shared platform learning legitimacy.

Extension is allowed. Weakening shared admission discipline is not.

## Failure Modes

Weakly governed evidence admission and threshold logic creates direct platform risk.

### Adaptation from incomplete episodes

The platform changes policy before recommendation, execution, outcome, and attribution lineage are fully reconstructible.

### Outcome headline learning

Recent outcomes trigger adaptation even though attribution remains weak or confounded.

### Scope-blurred learning

Evidence from unauthorized or commercially incomparable populations is admitted into policy-learning review.

### Override overlearning

Memorable or politically weighted override behavior distorts policy without repeated governed proof.

### Threshold collapse

Evidence that would once have justified monitoring only is allowed to trigger real policy change.

### Local flattening

Broad evidence is treated as sufficient to overwrite persistent local contradiction.

### False rigor

The platform claims that evidence is strong because many artifacts exist, even though they are not comparable, mature, or attributable enough for the proposed update.

### Unstable adaptation tempo

Policy changes too frequently because threshold discipline is too weak to resist short-term variation.

These failure modes are not mere statistical errors. They are ways the platform can become performatively adaptive while becoming less disciplined.

## Non-Negotiables

1. Policy must not change from evidence that is not admissible.
2. Evidence admission does not by itself justify policy update.
3. Stronger policy change requires stronger evidence.
4. Learning scope, decision scope, and reporting scope must remain distinct.
5. Attribution quality must be judged before adaptation is allowed.
6. Override evidence must not bypass the shared learning gate.
7. Cross-banner and cross-brand transfer is not valid by default.
8. Deferred adaptation is a legitimate governed outcome.
9. Weak-evidence protection must block anecdotal, noisy, or politically distorted adaptation.
10. Domains may extend threshold discipline, but they may not weaken it.

## Closing Statement

This document protects the platform from confusing interesting evidence with update-worthy evidence.

That protection matters because a decision system does not become intelligent merely by changing itself often. It becomes intelligent when it changes only where governed evidence justifies change and when it preserves the discipline to wait, narrow scope, or refuse adaptation where that discipline is the better decision.

If this standard remains intact, future domains can learn in different business settings while still sharing one platform-level rule for what evidence may enter policy learning and what strength that evidence must reach before future behavior changes.

If it weakens, the platform will start adapting faster than it can defend why.
