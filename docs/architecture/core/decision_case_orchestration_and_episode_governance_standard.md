## Why This Standard Exists

The platform's compounding edge depends not only on producing states, recommendations, reviews, routes, commitments, instructions, outputs, and closures, but also on disciplined control over how one case moves across those controlled layers as one reconstructible episode. Orchestration models sit between controlled decision objects on one side and recurring episode-level handling on the other. If orchestration meaning drifts quietly, the stack begins to trust visible case motion whose episode-level authority is weaker than it looks.

Episode stability is too weak by default. A decision episode can keep the same case shell and lose the same orchestration meaning. A handoff can remain visible and still fail legitimacy. An interruption can still be recorded and still stop preserving the same episode. A resumption can still look orderly and still fail legitimacy. A fallback can still move a case somewhere safe-looking and still fail governed reuse. If the platform cannot state what a governed decision episode means, what a governed orchestration model means, what makes episode entry and episode exit legitimate, what makes a handoff legitimate, what makes interruption and resumption legitimate, what fallback remains semantically faithful, and how later runs, domains, reviewers, routers, authorities, or learning-review surfaces may compare those orchestration models safely, then downstream trust weakens even while the operating flow still looks polished.

The platform therefore needs one shared standard so that orchestration models accumulate as governed capital rather than as a pile of locally useful but semantically unstable glue logic, workflow wrappers, queue choreography, and episode-handling ritual.

## Governed Decision Episodes and Orchestration Models

This standard governs the shared semantic control layer that sits between already-controlled decision-loop objects on one side and trusted reusable episode-handling structures on the other.

### Governed decision episode

governed decision episode is a governed case-wide orchestration meaning that states how one decision case remains one reconstructible handling episode across bounded entries, orchestrated handoffs, interruptions, resumptions, fallbacks, and exits within explicit scope, explicit entry basis, explicit handoff meaning, and explicit lineage strong enough for repeated serious use.

### Governed orchestration model

governed orchestration model is a governed episode-handling model that relates controlled decision-loop objects, state-bearing handling, routing-bearing handling, review-bearing handling, commitment-bearing handling, instruction-bearing handling, execution-bearing handling, and closure-bearing handling into legitimate episode-level motion under explicit orchestration paths, explicit handoff meanings, explicit interruption and resumption posture, explicit fallback rules, explicit scope, and explicit lineage strong enough for repeated serious use.

### Canonical orchestration definition

canonical orchestration definition is the authoritative governed definition that states what a governed decision episode or governed orchestration model means, what semantic scope it applies to, what entry basis supports it, what handoff, interruption, resumption, and fallback rules constrain it, what audiences it may support, and what semantic conditions must remain true for reuse to stay legitimate.

Local workflow notes, local automation graphs, and local queue choreography may remain useful, but they do not become governed decision episodes or governed orchestration models merely because they are repeated, persuasive, or operationally familiar. Governed reusable orchestration requires stronger identity, scope, entry discipline, handoff discipline, interruption discipline, resumption discipline, fallback discipline, and lineage than local workflow practice does.

## Episode Identity, Scope, and Audience

### Episode identity

episode identity is the stable identity linking one governed decision episode to its canonical orchestration definition, episode semantic scope, entry basis, handoff meaning, orchestration posture, intended audience, and later lineage rather than reducing it to a case ID, workflow run label, or local orchestration alias.

### Orchestration identity

orchestration identity is the stable identity linking one governed orchestration model to its canonical orchestration definition, orchestration paths, handoff rules, interruption and resumption posture, fallback rules, intended audience, and later lineage rather than reducing it to a workflow diagram, agent flow, process configuration, or orchestration engine label.

### Episode semantic scope

episode semantic scope is the explicit statement of what business meaning, decision-loop meaning, control boundary, orchestration boundary, and audience-relevant handling boundary a governed decision episode applies to and where that meaning must not be stretched by analogy or convenience.

orchestration models must have named scope, entry basis, and handoff meaning. A governed orchestration model that cannot state what kind of episode it structures, what allows episode entry, what handoff meaning it depends on, what interruptions and resumptions it treats as still belonging to the same episode, what audiences it is for, and what it does not authorize is too weak for canonical reuse.

Audience does matter, but audience fitting does not grant semantic freedom. An orchestration model may be rendered for operators, reviewers, domain authorities, execution-support surfaces, or observability-facing tooling, yet the underlying governed meaning must remain stable enough that later users can tell what the episode means and what it does not mean.

## Episode-Entry and Episode-Exit Legitimacy

### Episode-entry legitimacy

episode-entry legitimacy is the governed condition in which an episode-entry basis has named scope, named entry meaning, named owning surface, named initial orchestration claim, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell why a case was allowed to enter one governed decision episode rather than remaining only a set of disconnected controlled objects.

### Episode-exit legitimacy

episode-exit legitimacy is the governed condition in which an episode-exit basis has named scope, named exit meaning, named owning surface, named downstream handoff or closure relation, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell why an episode was allowed to leave active orchestration without erasing the meaning of what happened inside it.

episode presence is not the same thing as episode legitimacy. A visible episode shell may still be semantically illegitimate if its entry basis is unclear, its exit basis drifted, its handoff claims outran their underlying controls, or its orchestration interpretation expanded into adjacent standards.

Episode-entry legitimacy requires that the episode can state what kind of case it is for, what entry basis allows episode formation, what initial orchestration claim it makes, what controlled objects it expects to coordinate, and what kinds of cases must not enter it. Episode-exit legitimacy requires that the episode can state what basis allows active orchestration to end, what downstream closure or transfer relation it depends on, what kinds of interruptions still keep the same episode alive, and what kinds of departures require a different governed path. An episode that exists in software or habit without preserved episode-entry legitimacy and episode-exit legitimacy remains too weak for governed reuse.

## Orchestration Paths, Handoffs, Interruptions, and Resumptions

### Orchestration path legitimacy

orchestration path legitimacy is the governed condition in which an orchestration path preserves explicit step meaning, explicit cross-object relation, explicit handoff rules, explicit interruption and resumption posture, explicit fallback posture, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell what kind of episode-level motion the path expresses and what motion it does not express.

### Handoff legitimacy

handoff legitimacy is the governed condition in which an orchestration handoff preserves explicit sender meaning, explicit receiving meaning, explicit object-bearing relation, explicit authority-bearing relation where relevant, explicit downstream expectation, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell why the handoff belonged inside the same episode and what claim the handoff did not make.

### Interruption legitimacy

interruption legitimacy is the governed condition in which an orchestration model treats a pause, block, hold, quarantine-adjacent pause, review wait, or other bounded interruption as part of the same episode under explicit meaning, explicit boundary, explicit duration posture where relevant, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell why the interruption did not dissolve episode continuity.

### Resumption legitimacy

resumption legitimacy is the governed condition in which an orchestration model treats renewed handling after a legitimate interruption or separately permitted re-entry as still belonging to the same episode under explicit continuity meaning, explicit resumption basis, explicit object relation, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell why the resumed handling still belongs to the same episode.

### Fallback legitimacy

fallback legitimacy is the governed condition in which an orchestration-model fallback from an intended path to a stricter, earlier, more contained, or differently coordinated path remains explicit enough that later users can tell why fallback occurred, what boundary forced it, and why the fallback did not silently rewrite episode meaning.

an orchestration path is not the same thing as a state model by itself. State-transition governance owns state meaning and progression legitimacy. Progression-gate governance owns whether a case instance satisfied the relevant gate and whether a specific transition was entitled in episode history. This file governs how those already-controlled meanings are coordinated into one legitimate episode path rather than what those meanings are by themselves.

handoff visibility is not the same thing as handoff legitimacy. A visible handoff may still be semantically illegitimate if the sender and receiver meanings are unclear, the handoff silently crosses a different orchestration boundary, the receiving path was inferred rather than governed, or the handoff is being used to disguise object gaps that belong elsewhere.

handoff changes must remain explicit and lineage-safe. When a model changes orchestration path structure, handoff semantics, interruption semantics, resumption semantics, or fallback rules materially, that change must remain visible through orchestration lineage, episode lineage, orchestration audit trace, or all three rather than preserving stable labels as if nothing important changed.

orchestration models must not silently redefine review posture. orchestration models must not silently redefine escalation posture. An orchestration model may include review-bearing or escalation-bearing handoffs, but those edges remain subordinate to the standards that own review and escalation posture. The orchestration model may structure how those controlled layers fit into the episode. It does not own whether review or escalation as a control posture is legitimate in the first place.

interruption handling is not the same thing as orchestration legitimacy. A model may record a pause or resumption and still fail legitimacy if the episode boundary, handoff meaning, continuity basis, or fallback meaning drifted underneath stable labels.

### Episode legitimacy

episode legitimacy is the governed condition in which a governed decision episode has stable identity, named scope, named entry basis, named handoff meaning, named continuity posture, named exit basis, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell what kind of episode claim it expresses and what claim it does not express.

### Orchestration legitimacy

orchestration legitimacy is the governed condition in which a governed orchestration model has stable identity, named orchestration paths, named handoff rules, named interruption and resumption posture, named fallback posture, named episode meaning, and reconstructible lineage strong enough that later users can tell what model of episode coordination it expresses and what model it does not express.

### Episode lineage

episode lineage is the reconstructible chain linking episode identity, canonical orchestration definition, episode-entry basis, episode-exit basis, inherited or extended status, lifecycle status, invalidation or supersession where relevant, and later downstream use.

### Orchestration lineage

### Orchestration audit trace

a decision episode is not the same thing as a workflow by itself. local usefulness is not the same thing as canonical orchestration admission. ## Inheritance, Extension, and Comparability

### Inherited orchestration model

inherited orchestration model is a governed orchestration model reused without material semantic change from an earlier legitimate orchestration model whose identity and lineage remain explicit.

### Domain-extended orchestration model

domain-extended orchestration model is a governed orchestration model that extends an inherited orchestration model for a bounded domain need while keeping the extension explicit enough that comparability and semantic review remain possible.

### Comparability-safe orchestration pair

comparability-safe orchestration pair is a pair of governed orchestration models whose episode semantic scope, entry basis, handoff meaning, interruption and resumption posture, fallback posture, orchestration-path meaning, and lineage remain explicit enough that comparison is legitimate rather than inferred.

### Non-comparable orchestration pair

non-comparable orchestration pair is a pair of governed orchestration models whose episode semantic scope, entry basis, handoff meaning, interruption and resumption posture, fallback posture, orchestration-path meaning, or lineage differ materially enough that comparison must remain blocked or explicitly qualified.

### Promotion-safe orchestration use

promotion-safe orchestration use is orchestration-model use whose identity, semantic scope, entry basis, handoff meaning, orchestration legitimacy, interruption and resumption posture, lifecycle status, reuse posture, and lineage are explicit enough that it may be considered through stricter downstream gates without implying that broader canonical admission, router legitimacy, review legitimacy, state legitimacy, or learning admission has already been granted.

comparability is not the same thing as superficial orchestration similarity. Shared queue names, similar diagrams, similar step counts, similar dashboards, or similar interruption-looking paths do not by themselves make two orchestration models comparable.

inherited orchestration models must remain distinguishable from domain-extended orchestration models. A domain extension may narrow scope or add bounded local interpretation, but it must not silently widen episode meaning, silently change handoff meaning, silently change interruption or resumption posture, silently change fallback rules, or silently reuse the inherited label as if shared meaning remained unchanged.

canonical orchestration admission must be stricter than local operational usefulness. promotion-safe orchestration use must be stricter than local process convenience.

## Invalidation, Supersession, and Retirement

### Orchestration drift

orchestration drift is the governed condition in which an orchestration model's practical behavior, handoff meaning, interruption and resumption posture, fallback posture, or interpretive consequence shifts materially enough that later reuse may no longer be semantically safe.

### Superseded orchestration model

superseded orchestration model is an orchestration model whose current canonical role has been replaced by a later governed orchestration model while its historical identity remains visible and reconstructible.

### Deprecated orchestration model

deprecated orchestration model is an orchestration model whose new use is discouraged or bounded while its historical identity and limited transitional visibility remain active.

### Retired orchestration model

retired orchestration model is an orchestration model whose active governed use has ended while its historical existence and semantic trace remain reconstructible.

### Invalidated orchestration model

invalidated orchestration model is an orchestration model whose ordinary reuse is prohibited because orchestration legitimacy, episode-entry legitimacy, episode-exit legitimacy, handoff legitimacy, interruption legitimacy, resumption legitimacy, fallback legitimacy, comparability posture, or lineage posture has been broken materially enough that governed reuse is unsafe.

orchestration drift must remain explicit and reviewable. superseded orchestration models must remain historically identifiable. retired orchestration models must remain distinguishable from deprecated orchestration models.

Once an orchestration model is invalidated, superseded, deprecated, or retired, that lifecycle posture must remain visible strongly enough that later users cannot quietly treat it as current merely because software, documents, or orchestration tooling still reference it.

## Failure Modes and Anti-Patterns

### Reused orchestration-model name with changed meaning

An orchestration-model name may remain stable while episode meaning, handoff meaning, interruption posture, resumption posture, or fallback interpretation changes underneath it. That breaks legitimacy while falsely preserving apparent continuity.

### Orchestration path reused with different semantics

An orchestration path may survive across contexts even though the handoff semantics, interruption handling, resumption assumptions, or fallback meaning changed materially. That preserves familiarity while destroying semantic continuity.

### Orchestration model reused across non-comparable contexts

An orchestration model may be reused across teams, domains, or operational surfaces as though one visible episode shape or one familiar label proved comparability, even when the underlying orchestration pair is non-comparable.

### Inherited orchestration mistaken for domain-extended orchestration

Local extension may quietly impersonate inherited orchestration meaning, causing later users to assume shared episode semantics where only bounded local adaptation exists.

### Polished workflow mistaken for legitimate orchestration

Users may trust a workflow because it looks polished, efficient, or professionally packaged even though its episode meaning, handoff boundaries, or reuse posture are too weak for serious trust.

### Orchestration drift hidden under stable labels

Visible labels and familiar orchestration views may remain unchanged while meaning drifts materially underneath them, making stable naming a disguise for unstable orchestration semantics.

### Invalidated orchestration still used as current

An invalidated orchestration model may remain active in software, dashboards, runbooks, or operating habits even after governance has withdrawn it, leaving obsolete episode meaning in circulation.

### Local usefulness mistaken for canonical legitimacy

One useful local orchestration model may be treated as though repeated convenience proved governed canonical validity. That confuses operational utility with platform control.

### Lineage break between entry logic and handoff meaning

The orchestration model may lose reconstructible linkage between what allowed episode entry and what handoff meaning followed, leaving later users unable to tell why the episode ever looked legitimate.

### Silent mutation of orchestration interpretation

Interpretive notes, handoff guidance, interruption guidance, resumption guidance, or fallback meaning may change without explicit governance visibility, causing readers to trust a stable orchestration shape whose meaning no longer matches prior use.
